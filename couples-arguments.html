The argument that is never about the thing.

Hilary McKee Couples & Relationships 7 min read

Every couple has one. The dishes argument. The tone-of-voice argument. The you-were-five-minutes-late argument. The fight that keeps coming back wearing slightly different clothes, and that somehow always ends up in the same place no matter where it starts.

Here is the thing I want you to consider: that argument is never actually about the dishes. It is not about the tone, or the lateness, or whose turn it was. Those are just the doorways. What the argument is actually about lives underneath — and until a couple learns to work at that level, they can win the argument a hundred times and still lose the relationship.

I have been reading Terrence Real's book Us, and it has given me some of the clearest language I have found for what actually happens between two people in those moments. Three of his ideas in particular have stayed with me, and I want to share them here — because I think they change how you see every fight you have ever had.

Idea one: there is no objective truth to win

Most arguments between partners are, at their core, a battle over whose version of reality is correct. Who really started it. What you actually said. Whether that tone was rude or whether you are being too sensitive. We fight as if there is a neutral referee somewhere who could review the tape and declare a winner.

There isn't. Real calls this out directly: the search for objectivity in a relationship is a myth. You and your partner are two different nervous systems, with two different histories, standing in two different places. You did not experience the same moment. You never do. Your partner's version of what happened is not a distortion of the truth — it is their truth, filtered through everything they have lived.

This lands hard for a lot of couples, because being right feels so important in the moment. But here is the question that matters more than "who is right": do you want to be right, or do you want to be married? Not married in the legal sense — married in the sense of actually connected, actually on the same team. Because you often cannot have both at once. Winning an argument against your partner is winning an argument against your own team.

You can win the argument a hundred times and still lose the relationship.

Idea two: the adaptive child takes the wheel

So if the fight is not about the dishes, what is it about? Usually, it is about who just showed up in the room.

Real describes two parts of us that can be present in any moment of relational stress. He calls them the wise adult and the adaptive child. The wise adult is the grounded, present-day you — the one who can stay curious, hold complexity, and remember that the person across from you is someone you love. The adaptive child is the part of you that formed a long time ago, in your family of origin, to survive whatever needed surviving. It learned strategies: get louder, go silent, withdraw, control, appease, defend.

Those strategies were brilliant once. They protected you. But the adaptive child does not know the year has changed. When your partner's tone hits an old nerve, that part of you takes the wheel in milliseconds — before your thinking brain even catches up. And here is the crucial part: it is usually your adaptive child talking to their adaptive child. Two protective, activated younger selves, having a fight that neither present-day adult would choose.

The adaptive child

The part that learned to survive

Formed early, in response to what your family and environment required of you. It runs old protective strategies at full speed the moment it senses threat.

Black-and-white thinking — you always, you never Needing to win, be right, or be vindicated Stonewalling, withdrawing, or going cold Defending yourself before you have even listened

The wise adult

The part that can actually repair

The grounded, present-day you. It can feel hurt without becoming the hurt, and it remembers the relationship matters more than the point being argued.

Can hold your truth and theirs at the same time Gets curious instead of building a case Can pause, soften, and come back to repair Remembers you are on the same team

This is also where my nervous system writing connects. The adaptive child taking over is not a character flaw — it is a nervous system state. When your body registers threat, the parts of your brain responsible for perspective and empathy go partially offline, and the old survival strategies run the show. If you want to understand that mechanism more deeply, I wrote about it in Your body has three states.

Idea three: we were trained to think like a me, not an us

The third idea is the biggest one, and it is the heart of Real's book. Most of us — especially in Western culture — were raised inside a deep, mostly invisible individualism. Look out for yourself. Protect your interests. Stand your ground. Know your worth. None of that is wrong, exactly. But we carry that same operating system into our relationships, and it quietly wrecks them.

Because a relationship is not two individuals negotiating a contract. It is an ecosystem. You and your partner are not adversaries at a table — you are parts of one living system, and what you do to that system, you do to yourself. When you "win" by diminishing your partner, the us loses, and you live inside the us. When you protect yourself by withdrawing, the us gets colder, and you live inside that cold.

Real's invitation is to shift from thinking like a me to thinking like an us. Not by erasing yourself — this is not about self-abandonment or keeping the peace at any cost. It is about recognizing that in a healthy relationship, the question is rarely "how do I get what I need from you?" and much more often "what does the us need right now, and how do we both take care of it?"

That shift changes the fight about the dishes entirely. The question stops being "who is right about the dishes" and becomes "what is happening to us right now, and how do we get back to each other?"

So what do you actually do with this?

A few honest starting points — not fixes, but doorways.

Learn to notice whose hands are on the wheel. The single most useful skill here is catching the moment your adaptive child takes over. The clues are usually physical: heat in your chest, a tight jaw, the surge of needing to win. You do not have to fix it in the moment. Just noticing "this is the old part of me driving" is the beginning of choice.

Give up the referee. The next time you feel the pull to establish what really happened, try letting the question go entirely. Both of your experiences are real. Start there, and see what becomes possible.

Ask the us question. In the middle of tension, try asking — even just silently, to yourself — what does the us need right now? It is remarkable how often the answer is not "for me to win this."

And if this is the territory you and your partner keep getting stuck in, this is exactly what couples therapy is for. Not a referee to declare a winner — a place to learn how the two of you got wired the way you did, and how to come back to the same team.

If you want to go deeper

The ideas in this piece draw on Terrence Real's book Us: Getting Past You and Me to Build a More Loving Relationship (2022), which I highly recommend to anyone in a long-term relationship. His approach, Relational Life Therapy, is worth exploring at terryreal.com. For the research foundations of couples work more broadly, The Gottman Institute is another excellent resource.
